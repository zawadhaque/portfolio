About

The short version of who's behind the work.

The first time I broke something on purpose, I was hijacking a logged-in session with a few lines of cross-site script in a sophomore security course. It worked, and I sat there a little stunned that something so small, so hidden, could quietly hand me someone's account. I didn't want to just do it again. I wanted to know exactly why it worked.

That's the thing that pulls me through this field: the moment a system stops being a black box and you can see the machinery — what tripped the alert, where the packet really went, which assumption the attacker leaned on. I chase that aha, and I've been chasing it since I was a kid taking things apart just to understand how they held together.

I've pointed all of that at the defensive side, on purpose. A Cybersecurity & Information Assurance degree, hands-on BTL1 training in forensics, incident response, and SIEM investigations, research into mobile TLS trust and the security of large language models. I'm less interested in collecting tools than in the work underneath good defense: catching the thing that's slightly off, following it all the way down, and writing it up so the next person can see what I saw.

I'm headed for a SOC analyst seat now, with my sights set further out on purple teaming, because what draws me is seeing it from both sides: attacker and defender at once.

L-01 Privilege Escalation

Attacking Set-UID Programs

Worked through two attacks on privileged Set-UID programs. First I exploited a stack buffer overflow to inject shellcode and land a root shell, then re-ran it against address-space randomization, a non-executable stack, and StackGuard to see which countermeasures held and why. Then I traced how environment variables like PATH and the dynamic linker can quietly redirect a privileged program to attacker-controlled code, including capability leaking when it shells out to external commands.

Tools Buffer overflowShellcodeSet-UIDEnvironment variablesASLR / StackGuard
L-02 Web Security

Cross-Site Scripting Worm (Elgg)

Built a self-propagating XSS worm against a deliberately vulnerable build of the Elgg social network, in the spirit of the 2005 Samy worm. Injected JavaScript into user profiles to steal session cookies and auto-add the attacker as a friend, then turned on a Content Security Policy to watch the same attack fail.

Tools XSSJavaScriptSession cookiesAjaxCSP

R-01 Connected Vehicles

Cybersecurity in Transportation Systems

Co-authored a survey of 104 people on how everyday users perceive the security of connected vehicles and intelligent transportation systems. Weighed awareness against real concern: 74% saw connected vehicles as a significant privacy risk, yet only about a third knew the mobile apps controlling vehicle functions could be insecure.

Topics Connected vehiclesITSSurvey researchPrivacy
R-02 AI Security

Data Privacy Risks in Generative AI

Co-authored hands-on research into data leakage from large language models. We fine-tuned GPT-3.5 and GPT-4 on synthetic company data, then tried to pull it back out: GPT-3.5 gave up salaries and budgets in 12 of 20 trials once a request was framed as an audit, while GPT-4 held all 20. Documented prompt-leaking and access-control gaps, and proposed permission-scoped models as a fix.

P-02 Mobile Security

Mobile App TLS Trust Testing

Tested how mobile apps protect data in transit, from two angles. Dynamically, I stood up mitmproxy as a man-in-the-middle to inspect the live traffic of 10 apps across real iOS and Android devices: three failed, one over plain HTTP, one exposing login credentials in the URL, and one leaking credentials on Android while staying secure on iOS. Statically, I decompiled 10 cloud-storage Android apps and audited their Network Security Configuration files: none pinned certificates, and several still allowed cleartext traffic or user-installed certificates, leaving their traffic open to interception or tampering by an attacker on the same network.

Tools mitmproxyMITMTLSAPK decompilationNSCiOS / Android
